If you haven’t heard by now, the `.io`
<abbr title="country code top-level domain">ccTLD</abbr> is at risk of removal
due to a
<a rel="nofollow" href="https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/c98ynejg4l5o" title="UK will give sovereignty of Chagos Islands to Mauritius" target="_blank">
  British Treaty that was signed on October 3, 2024</a>.
In short, the Chagos Islands (ccTLD: `.io`) in the Indian Ocean have been given
to Mauritius (ccTLD: `.mu`); learn more by reading
<a rel="nofollow" href="https://every.to/p/the-disappearance-of-an-internet-domain" target="_blank" title="The Disappearance of an Internet Domain">
  The Disappearance of an Internet Domain</a>.
Of course, the Internet is buzzing because, according to policy, that would
mean `.io` has to be removed from the <abbr title="top-level domain">TLD</abbr>
registry.  Some people, however, are skeptical and say that too many websites
use `.io` and would be able to “save” it by throwing money at the
<a rel="nofollow" href="https://www.iana.org/" title="Internet Assigned Numbers Authority Homepage" target="_blank">
  Internet Assigned Numbers Authority</a>.

Personally, I wouldn’t bet money either way.  On one hand, standards exist for
a reason and shouldn’t be abandoned because companies ignore the risk.
Conversely, there seems to be a lack of education and enforcement of the
standards.  For example,
<a rel="nofollow" href="https://www.ionos.co.uk/digitalguide/domains/domain-extensions/cctlds-a-list-of-every-country-domain/#:~:text=Characteristics%20of%20ccTLDs,in%20Sinhalese%20and%20Tamil%2C%20respectively." title="Country domains: a comprehensive ccTLD list" target="_blank">
  Sri Lanka has three ccTLDs</a>
(albeit in different alphabets),
<a rel="nofollow" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/.su" title="Wikipedia: .su" target="_blank">
  The Soviet Union’s ccTLD, `.su`</a>,
still exists despite being dissolved in 1991, and GoDaddy
<a rel="nofollow" href="https://www.godaddy.com/tlds/co-domain" title=".co is global, recognizable — and moving fast" target="_blank">
  promotes ccTLDs like `.co`</a>
without ever mentioning that it’s supposed to represent Colombia.  In fact,
when talking about the `.io` domain,
<a rel="nofollow" href="https://www.godaddy.com/tlds/io-domain" title="Buy a .io domain and start something great" target="_blank">
  GoDaddy states</a>
the following:

> The `.io` domain extension is mainly used as an abbreviation for
Input/Output, which makes it very popular for tech startups in general.  The
`.io` TLD also represents the country code for the British Indian Ocean
Territory.

Note how the country representation is implied as secondary to “Input/Output.”
Without proper education from the registrars, it’s no wonder many companies are
caught flat-footed by this news.

That being said, I think this serves as a great case study for why following
standards will always be the best bet.  For those wondering, that means
anything with two characters represents a country, which should only exist as
long as the country exists, and anything with three or more characters is
generic, which should theoretically last “forever.”
